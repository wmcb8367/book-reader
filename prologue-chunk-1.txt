In 2016, at age 26, I coached Paris Henken—then just 20 years old—and Helena Scutt—24 at the time—at the Rio Olympics, making me the youngest coach for the US Olympic Sailing Team in several decades. At age 34, I still get asked how I could possibly have coached at the Olympics so young.

The answer starts with Ty Reed. Ty was my high school coach, and the one who introduced me to the 29er. I remember when he asked, "Do you want to keep sailing the Laser? Because if you start sailing the 29er, you're never going to want to go back." He was right. I did my time in the C420, Laser, and FJ, but they were all just stepping stones to what I really cared about: skiff sailing. I sailed the 29er through high school, representing the U.S. at the ISAF Youth Worlds, and by college I was running clinics to pass on the knowledge I had gained from training with teammates like Oliver Toole and our friends and rivals Judge Ryan and Hans Henken.

That desire to share turned into something bigger: the Skiff Squad. It started small, but it grew fast. Our first BootCamp brought together names that would soon become a big part of the U.S. sailing scene: Dane Wilson, Chris Williford, Riley Gibbs, Tucker Atterbury, and others who would go on to compete in college and beyond. The group dominated the national 29er circuit, with consistent podiums at the Youth Worlds and top finishes at 29er Worlds.

The secret wasn’t me. It was the system: a generational handoff of knowledge. Most sailors started as helms and aged into crewing roles, helping a younger helm along the same path. Each generation learned from the one before, added their unique learnings, and then passed that knowledge down. It created a pipeline.

The 29er class has a hard age limit of 18 for the Youth Worlds. That means most sailors have a 2-3 year window as a helm, maybe another 2-3 as a crew. The basic skill set can be taught in a year, refined in the second, and by the third, it’s all about racing. Transitioning to crew brings fresh focus, and mentoring a younger helm becomes part of the challenge. Skiff Squad became a place to refine that process.

While I was in college, I ran Skiff Squad on the side. It was scrappy. 29ers were expensive. Coach boats were even more expensive. Some days I lost money because teams canceled last-minute, but it taught me how to run a program, manage people, and share a vision.
