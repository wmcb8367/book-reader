One of my coaches early in my sailing career had a saying that he liked to repeat whenever we talked about tactics and strategy: he would say, "Sailing is simple. You only ever have one decision to make: do I tack, or do I go straight?" 

Some of the sailors who I've coached have also been coached by Ed Baird, who likes to say that, "When two boats cross, one of them is making the wrong decision and one is making the right decision, so if you're one of the two, you need to figure out which one you are." 

In making decisions around the race course, there are three main building blocks that you need to understand in order to make good choices. The first of these building blocks is what I'm going to refer to as **Race Course Philosophy**. Race Course Philosophy is the idea that whether your race course is set in Santa Barbara, California, Miami, Florida, or Auckland, New Zealand, there are some characteristics of the boat that you're racing that will always apply in your decision making. 

Take the 49er class for instance. Like many classes in sailing, the International 49er Class Association provides guidelines to race organizers to help them run good races. Target times for the length of a race are generally 35 minutes, with a matrix of course lengths corresponding to wind strengths to help race committees set good courses. If you know the course length, and you know the approximate wind speed, you can work backwards from there to understand how long the first beat will be, how long you are likely to spend on starboard tack before reaching layline and even what percentage of your overall leverage you stand to take by starting at a particular end of the starting line. If a lot of that went over your head, don't worry, we'll break it all down in the next topic in the course, but for now, what's important to understand is that those factors all fall into this idea of Race Course Philosophy, because they are factors that will generally be predictable no matter where in the world you're racing, how shifty the wind is, or what tack you're on at any given moment. 

The second major building block that you'll need to understand is **Strategy**. I like to think of strategy as your roadmap for a race. To create a good roadmap, you'll need to understand what the weather is on a macro level, as well as how the wind and current are moving on a more micro level. For this section, we're going to talk a lot about different types of days, and I'm going to share the system that I've developed by categorizing and combining the best ideas of the top coaches that I've worked alongside. 

Finally, we'll dig into the one-on-one knife fights that occur around the race course, in the topic on **Tactics**. This section will be all about the nuances of positioning and the tradeoffs that we make when we use our leverage to create big, comfortable lanes, or to put pressure on our competitors. Here we'll discuss how to balance wind prediction - sailing just against the wind without regard to what your competitors are doing - with fleet management - how you respond to competitor position.

An interesting piece of race course philosophy in the 49er fleet is that on average, the top boats only pass about two boats per race after the top mark. This means that if you can learn to win the one-on-one match races around the course, and pick the fights you know you have a good chance of winning, you can minimize risks that a lot of competitors take when they feel that they need to bet big to make gains. Instead of betting on factors that are outside of your control, we'll talk about how to consistently pick off a few boats each race while maintaining high percentage strategic execution. 

Understanding the big picture will help you separate these topics in your mind, and organize your thoughts into a more systematic process that you can tweak later on. Ultimately it will shape your mindset to help to know at any moment whether you should tack or go straight!
