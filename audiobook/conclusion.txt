### When The Unconscious Becomes Conscious

On the race course, decision making often boils down to a gut feeling and an intuitive reaction in the moment. It can feel spontaneous, and magical - like a sixth sense for sailboat racing. But underneath these intuitions - behind the curtain - is a complex process that your brain is constantly performing whether you realize it or not. One of the Kite Foil athletes that I have worked with loves the quote by psychologist Carl Jung: ​​"Until the unconscious becomes conscious, it will direct your life and you will call it fate." In the case of race course decision making, if we can break down the components of this mystical "sixth sense," we can start to understand the mechanics behind our intuition, and rebuild a new process for decision making based on measurements and logic instead of a conglomeration of random experiences.

In this book we've drilled down into the mechanics that drive this decision making process. We've highlighted some big picture factors of Race Course Philosophy that rarely change for individual racing disciplines. We've outlined five categories that wind patterns might fit into - how to predict those days, and ultimately how to adapt our strategies to fit them. And finally, we've looked at some tools that teams need to carry in their tactical repertoires to adapt for other boats on the race course so that the strategic aims can be realized.

Hopefully one of your takeaways here is that while we've presented a framework for each of these topics, the work is not complete. With today's technology, we have more data to analyze racing than ever before. GPS tracking will continue to push our understanding of high percentage race course decisions. Wind measurement systems will improve our ability to categorize conditions. For teams (likely at the Federation level) who invest in this research, and who are willing to evolve from traditional wisdom, there is a new frontier of tactical advantage that will produce sharper, more confident athletes. For teams who can implement this objective feedback into their process without losing sight of the intuitive, creative, feel-based decisions, there is an opportunity to learn faster than ever before.

Once this model begins to take shape however, we have one more task: putting it all together.

**No Mind.**

If we can make the unconscious conscious by drilling down into individual pieces, hopefully your experience on the race course feels less like fate and more like individual agency. But sometimes this same process that can be such a critical tool for expediting our learning can work against us.

In the movie, The Last Samurai, there's a ridiculous scene that I love, where Tom Cruise is learning to fight with a Samurai sword, and after practicing all of the moves, he starts sparring with some of the other samurai. For several rounds, he tries to use the moves that he has practiced, but time and time again, the other warriors slap his wooden sword aside, and connect with his face, ending the match in a few moves. One of the younger samurai who is watching comes to Cruise's rescue, counseling the dejected warrior after he finds himself on the ground yet again.

"Too many mind," says the character.

At first Tom doesn't understand what he means at first, but then the young samurai elaborates: "Mind sword. Mind the people watching. Mind enemy. Too many mind... No mind."

And suddenly Tom understands: he is thinking about all of the details of his training, and by getting lured into the details, he keeps getting schooled.

Queue inspirational music.

As Cruise stands up, he clears his mind. The first strike comes, and he responds fluidly - mindlessly - executing what he has practiced and flowing into the next movement, and then the next, and the next. 

Okay, I know it's kind of corny, but there is so much going on in this scene that I love, and I think it represents one of the biggest challenges that we face as athletes and coaches.

During training, we talk about the importance of the details. We break things down into tiny pieces and strive at every turn to understand not just WHAT works, but WHY it works. In this book we have gone deep into details of wind mechanics, tactical decision making and race course strategy that is likely far more detailed than any discussion you have had on the subjects. In my experience as a coach, dissecting the concepts like this creates a far more efficient way to learn very hard ideas.

But once you have explored these ideas in a critical way, we need to move them from the analytical realm to the intuitive. We need to think about the ideas long enough that they can percolate through our minds, and then trust that they will impact our split second responses. We need to move from an adult's analytical approach, to a child's fearless mindset of "no mind.

\***

An exercise that I like to use to analyze decision making with teams that I'm coaching, is to show them a clip of drone video and point to a boat: "You're dropped into this situation - what do you do from here?" In the classroom, we discuss positioning relative to other boats. We discuss wind that we see on the water. We discuss the big picture of where the boat is on the race course, and how many options are left. We try to weigh the importance of each of these factors, often categorizing them in terms of meters gained or lost to try to compare options. But on the water, we don't have this luxury.

In the heat of battle, every moment is a decision - either to continue straight, or to start executing a maneuver. Every decision requires the right balance of strategy, tactics and race course philosophy. Without thinking about it, each decision that you make on the race course draws from ideas in all three of those categories, and the x factor is how you balance them.

Some people call this talent. Others realize that this skill comes from experience, but they don't realize that experience alone isn't enough. Without the conceptual underpinnings in the back of your mind, reps on the race course aren't enough to produce this skill set. Equipped with the knowledge that you've picked up by working through this course, you now have the conceptual underpinnings floating around in your mind, and if you can pair these ideas with reps on the race course, you'll begin to find balance between these ideas in a way that will produce the x factor.

The trick here is to not get too fixated on any single idea. This is
easier said than done.

Armed with your new knowledge on types of day, it can be tempting to get on the course and focus all of your attention on the one or two factors that you think will be important on a given day. The pitfall here is that it's too easy to get tunnel vision, and miss big opportunities on the starting line, or miss changes in the weather.

In tight tactical situations, it's easy to focus on winning the boat-on-boat battle, but if this takes you to the wrong part of the race track, then you and your competitor will both lose out.

One way that I like to address this issue is to work very hard in the
mornings before racing, doing the homework, and then once the picture
has been painted, relax and tell some jokes as we get on the water. This
ensures that we are prepared with all of the necessary information, but
loose and relaxed going into racing.

Ultimately, once you have done your homework, letting things flow and trusting your gut is the final step that will allow you to develop a winning mindset over time.

\***

For some people, the analytical realm is the comfortable place to be. They're happy getting deeper and deeper into details, picking things apart more and more, to build a theoretical masterpiece... but often these people will have trouble executing what they have built when the pressure is on. If this is you, the final step after you've done the work of reading and thinking hard, is to turn your brain off, and go place yourself in difficult situations, where you can let the academic lessons become intuitive ones. If you're looking for more guidance in this area, I highly recommend reading the book, "The Inner Game of Tennis," or [listening to Michael Lewis' podcast with the author, Timothy Galloway.](https://podcasts.apple.com/ca/podcast/against-the-rules-with-michael-lewis/id1455379351?i=1000475065232)

Others live in the intuitive realm, and if that is you, congratulations on making it through this book! I hope that you have slowed down with the reading enough to really soak in the information, engage in the thought process, and make these ideas your own. For these people, it will be easier to switch back into competition mode, but more difficult to adapt and improve - especially at the top level. If this is you, it's worth reminding yourself frequently that "talent" only gets you so far, and that you need to put in the analytical work in the off seasons to ensure that you're bringing new ideas into your competition season.

While "talent" and "natural ability" are popular narratives from club level racing all the way up to the Olympics, there is no substitute for deep thinking, many hours of training to make these ideas second nature, and then reps around the race course learning to trust your intuition. The balance that we are looking to strike, and the process that we need to build, is one which leverages a deep analytical understanding of the decision making process to make improvements, but then allows these ideas to flow naturally on the race course without over analyzing.

There is no magic bullet; talent (nature) can't be separated from the learning process (nurture); and in our sport, there are as many different ways to build a successful program as there are teams. At the end of the day, the time that you've dedicated to this process of learning by reading this book will help these ideas percolate into your decision making on the race course, help you trust your gut feelings more, and give you more confidence as a person, as an athlete, and as a racer.

See you on the race course!
