*Day two of the 2020 49er World Championship was do or die for Paris Henken and Anna Tunnacliffe-Tobias in the 2020 Olympic Trials. On day one, a field of seaweed had blown across the course as the afternoon pressure filled, making it impossible to get around the course without collecting bouquets of floating weed on both foils. Teams experimented with different techniques and timings to try to clear blades with minimal losses, but it was experimentation under pressure. Regardless of how well you could clear, there was always that aspect of random bad luck if you hit a patch of weed on starboard layline, and had to gybe out before rounding the weather mark as a result. As the day went on, the kelp got thicker, and the scorelines became more erratic. By the end of day one, Paris and Anna sat in the high teens with 42 points total - 23 points behind Steph Roble and Maggie Shea, whom they needed to beat.*

*As teams tuned up for day two of racing, the team knew that they needed a big day to get back into contention, and from the coach boat, I knew that we didn't have any races to spare to start the comeback. Despite the big task ahead, confidence was high, and we hit the water focused on executing our routine.*

*An hour before racing started, we arrived on the race course to find nice breeze, and got to work tuning the rig for the full power, flat water conditions. One solid upwind, one solid downwind, a few turns on the rig, some small adjustments to sheeting ranges and techniques got the boat dialed for the day. And then we set about focusing on the conditions - what type of day would it be, and where would the advantage come from?*

*Unlike the usual thermal breeze that locals had told us to watch for, this breeze was gradient driven, with puffy, shifty streaks of pressure randomly moving down the course.*

*First, the randomness in itself held a critical piece of information: lay lines would be risky, but edges of the fleet would create opportunity.*

*The course was set against a thin peninsula of land obstructing the left corner of the course, and a shoreline a few hundred meters to weather of the windward mark. While the left land was closer, it was also quite low lying, whereas the right land to weather of the course had some large hills and more ground cover - a factor that Wind Strategy (Campbell, Houghton) would say might increase friction.*

*With the weather shoreline, we might expect more right shift at the top of the course in the Southern Hemisphere, but with the tall hills, this might also be accompanied by less pressure - something we observed in the warm up before racing.*

*Current was minimal in the bay, but a channel ran across the bottom of the course, diagonally from right to left, so if any water was flowing out in the ebb tide, it might have a small effect on sea state or speed over ground in the bottom third of the course.*

*With a preliminary mental model of the race course devised, we spent the last few minutes testing our theories, checking angles off of the pin, checking pressure in the left hand corner, and getting as many compass numbers on port and starboard tack as possible.*

*From the first starting gun, Paris and Anna were on a mission, executing a pin third start to edge out to the left side of the course. Off the line they carved out a nice hole, and then put their heads down in the boat for the first minute, focusing 100% on boat speed. A minute into the race their pack was looking good, so the discussion onboard went something like this: "High mode to peel the Danes off of our hip - then as soon as they go we're looking for a nice spot to tack with a big lane. Want to be able to hold for a while."*

*The scoreline tells the story of the next three races: their 1, 4, 1 won the day, launching them forward in the standings and setting the stage for the remainder of the event.*

Unlike football, basketball, or soccer where the strongest team on a given day can usually fight through the competition to carry the day, sailboat racing is a unique game of probabilities, wherein sometimes you do everything wrong and still win, and other times you do everything right only to be disappointed. In studying tactics and strategy, our challenge is to learn how to separate the outcomes from the decision making process.

Races are often won and lost on big features that change quickly, and are obvious in retrospect: a big shift that defines a beat, or a pressure line that rolls through and helps one side come out way ahead. The storyline as told by race winners often goes like this: "We looked up the course and saw pressure on one side, so we went there and we were right!" This is how many of us learn strategy in junior sailing: look upwind, trust your eyes, and bet on what you see. But what carries an individual race, often is not the same thing that carries the day, or wins a regatta.

What the best strategists tune into on any given day, are the underlying factors and conditions of the weather, the geography, the fleet, and the course that hint at longer term, smaller margin advantages.

The better the fleet you race in, the more everyone will identify the low hanging fruit, so the more nuanced the understanding must be to extract small gains. In a local fleet, identifying new pressure might be enough to give you the edge, but if everyone is sharp, the whole fleet will tune into the pressure differences. In the more advanced fleet, everyone might bet correctly on the side to play, but as the front row heads to that favored side and the wind oscillates slightly, the question often arises: is it more important to tack on the shift, or to head toward slightly better pressure? Many in the fleet will see this as a 50:50 chance, but top strategists have a system to help answer this question so that they are right 60 or 70 percent of the time. 

To add another layer of complexity to the previous example, if the starting line is biased towards the unfavored side, the odds of the favored side paying off might become even tighter. 

One of the reasons that we love sailboat racing is the dynamic, strategic game that changes every day, in every condition. The ever changing race course and tactical positioning against other boats makes every situation unique, and every tactical decision needs to be evaluated instantaneously as positions change. Dave Ullman likes to say, "Sailing is not a thinking game, it is a reaction game." In the tactical world where an inch or two difference in positioning can shift the balance of who is strong and who is weak, this is exactly right. In the McBride Racing Tactical Playbook, I wrote about some of the most common positioning scenarios, and the communications that set the stage for solid, split second reactions. In the final section of this book I will review some of these ideas as they relate to the execution of strategy, however the main focus of the first two parts of this book will be on big picture decision making. 

The goal of this book is to frame the decision making process in terms of a finite set of slowly changing factors, which can be evaluated ahead of time to identify small margin advantages over long periods of time. The discussion will start by focusing on **Race Course Philosophy** - the biggest of big picture factors, which change rarely if ever for a given class in a particular wind speed. The next section will dig into **Strategy,** building a framework for evaluating the "betting odds" on any given race course, and establishing five mindsets that should be applied to help prioritize the right factors across different conditions. Finally, we'll finish by discussing **Tactical positoning** and how your team can develop a playbook of moves that can be used to adapt to the quickly changing scenarios that develop with other boats on the race course. 

To put this all into a real world example, consider Paris and Anna's second day of the 2020 Worlds. Paris' Race Course Philosophy was something that had been built over years of 49er racing. She knew that the beat would be about 7 minutes long, and that she'd need to focus on speed for the first few minutes off the line. The Strategy was devised before the start of the race based on the geography of the surrounding area and the conditions that we saw in the forecast and verified on the race course. This gave us confidence that over the course of three races, the left would hold a slight edge over the right. The Tactical consideration was the discussion about peeling the boat on their hip through a high mode and then taking a big lane back across to the right. 

When these three pieces come together, the decision making process takes into account big picture priorities of the class you're racing, medium term factors of weather and geography that will help to know in advance which priorities are most important on a given day, and short term factors like puffs, lulls, positioning against other boats, etc. 

Hopefully this book will help you to bring these three factors together in your own sailing.
