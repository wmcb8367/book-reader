### What Are Tactics?

*With a minute and a half to the final start of the 2022 J70 World Championship, points were tight at the front of the fleet. All the players were stacked up, jockeying for position at a very favored pin end, and the seconds seemed to drag on as we got closer and closer to the pin boat and the layline that would force us to bail out.*

*"One minute to go." We were still okay.*

So far in this book we've discussed big picture factors of decision making including Race Course Philosophy and Strategy, but no matter how good you are at analyzing the type of day and figuring out where the marginal advantages might lie, your ability to execute on those ideas often comes down to split second actions that have to be automatic. Dave Ullman likes to say, "Sailing is not a thinking game, it's a reaction game." In the tight situations, there's not enough time to think - you just have to react. The study of tactics is all about creating an intuition about the boats, conditions and situations unfolding around you, that allow you to make the right response before the window of opportunity closes and the situation changes.

Two months before the J70 World Championship, the top tacticians in the world lined up at the 202One Tokyo Olympics, and my whole J70 team gathered around the television to watch and learn. In race one, we were already taking notes. Pete Burling and Blair Tuke - Olympic favorites and eventual silver medalists - lined up on starboard tack, outside of the port layline to the pin end of the starting line with a minute to go to the first start of their Gold Medal defense. As the time ticked down, they sat on starboard, wide of the line while the whole fleet battled with each other. At 30 seconds, we couldn't believe that they were still on starboard, pointed away from the line. At 20 we thought they must be making a mistake. Not until 10 seconds did they tack to port, and even then, they didn't accelerate towards the line - they just waited patiently. The gun went off, and still Pete and Blair were patient, waiting for the fleet to flop to port on a very pin favored line. As it turns out, the pin had been so favored, that simply by waiting for the gun to go off, and the fleet to clear out, they were able to snag a top five position of of the starting line, and two minutes into the race were battling for the lead. 

Flash back forward to the 2022 J70 Worlds.

*"50 seconds. Bogey behind you." Uh oh - need to defend - bow down hard.*

*"40 seconds. Not making it!"*

*As soon as we realized that we weren't laying the pin end, there was no time to think - only time to react.*

*"Pete and Blair Olympic race 1 start - do it." I didn't know what would happen next - if anyone would have any idea what I meant, but immediately everyone sprang into action. The jib came on hard and the bow came down low to create space for a tack. As soon as we were beyond layline, Jordan put the helm down to whip the boat around 180 degrees with the jib fully luffing. And then we waited... In front of us the fleet was in disarray.*

*We waited more. Boats who weren't laying started tacking onto port and starboard tackers who couldn't avoid, screamed as rails locked and gelcoat flew.*

*The starting gun went off and we waited a little bit more. Plan C boats started bailing out and suddenly there were several holes in front of us. We trimmed in and shot through a hole, eyes searching for our escape lane onto starboard.*

*Ten seconds passed and we ducked a starboard tacker with a nice line on their hip. We tacked into the upgraded lane, now going full speed, in clear air with only a few boats to the left of us who had started ahead.*

*Thirty seconds passed in this lane, and suddenly we were in clear water, racing hard against the top five in the fleet. We led back to the right near layline, and slowly inched away from the competition to take the bullet in the final race of the regatta.*

In that final race, we sailed like we had nothing to lose. We fought hard for the side that we wanted at the favored end, and took a lot of risk in doing so. It could have gone really poorly, but at the end of the day, the difference maker was a split second assessment and reaction to the positioning of boats around us that allowed us to execute a solid escape at the favored end, in a high-risk, high-consequence position and it all came together.

The study of tactics is about developing an intuitive sense of high percentage positioning based on where you want to go, and what unique situation presents itself. No two situations are exactly alike, but patterns can help you understand where to look for opportunity, and how different positioning decisions will impact the boats around you.

Tactics deserves a book of its own and in the McBride Racing Tactical Handbook, I've laid out a framework for a more complete study of the topic. In the book, I define tactics as: **Momentary deviations from the pre-planned strategy, which allow you to continue executing that strategy from the most effective position possible.**

In the future I plan to update that work and build an online curriculum around it. In this book, the tactical discussion will specifically focus on how we can use positioning to help execute our gameplans in the five types of days that we outlined in the strategy section. We'll talk about subtleties of lane selection, terminology to help clarify communications around positioning, and how to assess risk in positioning.

Ultimately the goal is to help you start to think about the push and pull of positioning on boats around you. The better you get at this skill, the more you will be able to **"read the minds" of your competitors**, and position yourself in lanes that will encourage them to do what you want them to do, whether that's bailing out of a lane early, consolidating with the fleet, or picking a fight with another boat.

Let's dive in.

### Tactics of Connect The Dots

On connect the dots days, the number one mantra is, "Sail your own race." Pressure is localized, so what you feel and the opportunities that you have access to are different from any other boat on the course. You might see big pressure on one side of the course, but if you can't get there, it doesn't matter - you have to **do what you can with what you have.**

How does this impact our tactical positioning around other boats? The main answer is that we need to maintain our ability to make our own decisions while closing down options for others. Getting pinned from tacking can be disastrous, because if the boat preventing you from tacking is in better pressure than you, there will be nothing you can do to upgrade your position.

Starting on port can be a big weapon here because it immediately gets you out of sync with the rest of the fleet, and makes it harder for other boats to control you. Low density starts and boat end starts are good for similar reasons - freedom to tack is everything.

On one connect the dots day of racing, debriefing with the 49er fleet at the Oakcliff Sailing Center in Oyster Bay, one of the sailors remarked, "We always wanted to round the top mark just behind the leaders. We would follow them until they started to run out of pressure at which point they had to point higher, and we would roll into a gybe immediately forcing them to extend straight into the lull for a few seconds, while we stayed in the puff."

If you're the boat ahead in this case, it's worth learning from the boat behind! They're looking for signs of lighter pressure to inform their gybe, so if you feel the pressure lightening, and put the bow up in response, you're telegraphing the change, and giving them time to set up for an easy jump. On the other hand, if you have the discussion ahead of time, that at the first sign of lighter pressure the skipper is going to turn the boat to gybe, both team members can be poised so that when pressure fades, rather than going bow up to build pressure, you let the pressure fade, the boat heel to weather and use this weather heel to roll straight into a gybe.

In response, maybe the boat behind changes their plan to a simultaneous gybe rather than gybing when you go bow up, but now you can defend by gybing to a hot angle - on my teams we use the term, “Gybe to hot.”

This game of cat and mouse is how the tactical game works, so learning to read the mind of your competition to understand where they think the opportunities lie is very important!

### Tactics of The Inside Track

One mantra that I like to keep in mind when executing the inside track strategy is, "Lead back lead packs."

In the section on Edge Out Tactics we'll talk about when to take fat lanes, and when to take thin lanes, but in contrast, the inside track usually benefits from taking "average" lanes - not so big that it tempts your competitors to take it, but not so small that you're forced to tack early, or forced to take losses by sailing in worse air than your closest competitors.

When we're sailing the inside track strategy, there are a few rules of thumb that can help with lane selection and shift recognition that I'll illustrate in a series of examples below. Each example is from the McBride Racing Tactical Playbook, and presents a hypothetical scenario, followed by a rule of thumb and description of the discussion and decision making process surrounding the scenario.

Take a look, decide how you would respond to these setups, and then read about the approach that we would recommend.

#### UW 5.1 -- Cross When You Can

**Guiding Principle(s):** Inside track.

**The Play:** In a steady oscillating breeze, the minimum distance around the course is to tack each time the wind shifts through the average wind direction, however, in practice, it can often times be difficult to know where that average direction is, and even if you know what it is, tactically there is often a higher percentage move: cross when you can. This nugget of wisdom that coaches often preach, ticks off a lot of different boxes on the strategic checklist. First of all, crossing packs when you have the opportunity often helps to put you in phase if you are uncertain of the shift pattern. If this is the case, chances are, much of the pack on your hip will end up tacking also. Probably more importantly, crossing when you can, establishes control over the boats around you. Putting you in a strong position to fleet manage the pack that you have just crossed. If the fleet does not tack to match your play, consider hipping back up on the fleet to loose cover and minimize leverage as you sail into the next shift.

In the scenario pictured, Red has gained enough of an advantage (likely from a small right shift) that she can tack without the threat of being lee-bowed by any of the boats on her hip. Using the boats on your hip as an indicator of whether or not a wind shift has occurred is a very good way to stay in phase. On port, the play applies in the same circumstances, however, you would need a bigger shift to tack and cross because you don't have right of way to force your competitors to avoid you.

**Most Common Mistakes:** Applying this play if it takes you out of pressure or into traffic.

**Pro Tip:** To defend against this, competitors will often tack simultaneously to prevent you from consolidating gains. Pair this play with UW 4.1 to close leverage and lock in gains when you are in a strong shift.

**Best Communication:** "We can cross the boats on our hip," "Let's consolidate," "Center up on this pack"

### UW 6

**Description:** In an oscillating breeze you are on the outside of a lift, and you believe that the next shift will be a header.  What is the high percentage move?

#### UW 6.1 -- Consolidate Gains By Footing In Lifts

**Guiding Principle(s):** Inside track.

**The Play:** VMG is typically independent of phases or shifts, but consolidating your lead when you are in a strong shift eliminates the need to time shifts perfectly, keeps options open, and becomes a higher and higher percentage option, as the breeze becomes less predictable. To execute this play, take advantage of lifts by sailing a low, fast mode to get over top of your competition as they get lifted into you. Essentially, you are burning some of your (recently extended) lead in order to close leverage, and assume a more dominant position over your competition.

The benefits of this play are obvious when a shift puts you out in front of your competition, but you can leverage the same idea even in close quarters, by footing into a controlling position where you can pin your opponent from tacking.

**Most Common Mistakes:** Footing too aggressively and ending up in bad air!

**Pro Tip:** In unstable, unpredictable conditions, this play paired with a tiny speed edge makes for a super powerful combination. Use your speed edge to stay alive when you feel weak, and then consolidate your lead when you feel strong against your competition. This tactics will create a lot of options down the road.

**Best Communication:** "Fast mode here," "Consolidate with a fast mode," "Kill mode"

### UW 7

**Description:** You are approaching the lead pack out of the left and you are nearly even with Green and Yellow. Your strategy is to play the inside track. Should you play through, or lead back?

#### UW 7.1 -- Lead Back Leading Packs

**Guiding Principle(s):** Inside track.

**The Play:** The dialogue that should be initiated at position one or earlier is, "Are we playing through or leading back?" In an oscillating breeze, this discussion should always occur before every crossing situation. In this case, favored side does not matter because this is the lead pack from the left, and they are all on port. Red should get as close to the leaders as possible, in order to shut down lanes, and make it difficult for Green to sail his own groove. Leading back is the high percentage move here both in terms of wind prediction as well as fleet management. The lead packing being on port is a good indicator that we might be in left phase, which means that the next shift will be back right. Even if the fleet is not a good indication of the phase, tacking to leeward puts Red in a position to consolidate any gains by tacking first, should the breeze go right. Finally, if Red is bow behind at all, leading back saves any potential ducking situations. If left pressure begins to come in hard, it is likely that Green will tack to minimize Yellow's leverage, in which case Red will be able to go as well. This also preserves starboard advantage.

**Most Common Mistakes:** Don't get greedy, and try to force the fleet to tack just because you are a Starboard boat. Chances are, one or two boats from the pack will find a way to get to the right of you (either by ducking, or by crossing you), which gives them starboard advantage and control of the right shift.

**Pro Tip:** The deeper you are in the fleet, the earlier this conversation needs to take place. If half of the port pack is crossing you, you need to know early on whether your plan is to play through or lead back so that you can tack early enough to keep your bow clear.

**Best Communication:** "Lead back or play through?" "Leading back," "Tacking to leeward of the pack"

### Tactics of The Edge Out Strategy

When it comes to learning how to execute the Edge Out strategy well, one of the most challenging aspects of these types of day is that often the rich get richer. If you can escape from the line and make the first decision correctly, you set yourself up to control the fleet, making the next move easier, and the move after that even easier. Get it wrong however, and it will be an especially difficult fight to get back into the race. As a result we're going to start with a discussion about the first tactical decision off the line - the timing of the first tack relative to other boats - and then we'll build from there.

The following illustration shows the importance of this first tack timing. In these three images, Red, Purple, and Green have started to leeward of Blue, and have created a half boat length advantage one minute after the start. All three are looking to tack.

Red is not sure where she wants to go, but after seeing Blue decide to tack, matches the maneuver a second later.

*Figure 45. Subtleties in the leeward boat's tack position can make a big difference tactically.*

Purple knows she wants to tack, and communicates that she wants a "simultaneous tack" with Blue, turning at the same time and maintaining the original distance.

Green knows that she is looking to tack and pressure Blue, so the moment she sees Blue prepping sheets for the tack, she starts cheating into the turn to be one step ahead.

The end result is that Green can put the bow down to pressure Blue, but may need to tack away fairly quickly if Blue gains an advantage. Red has a big lane to live in, which may be good if she expects to go straight for a long time, but doesn't put much pressure on Blue, and is at a disadvantage if she expects a right shift next, because she has given Blue additional leverage. Purple maintains the same leverage, which gives her tactical options in the next shift.

While the Red position is likely good for a Place To Race To/Outside Track race, or a Minimize Decisions strategy because it will allow Red to hang for a long time, Purple is likely better for the Inside Track strategy because she will be able to live through some small shifts, while having the option of a tight leebow on the next shift. Green is ideal for an Edge Out strategy where Green started close to the pin. In this case, pressuring Blue back to the middle of the course takes away their ability to sail a high mode tactically or for VMG reasons, and forces them to sail quickly back towards the rest of the fleet.

*Figure 46. A real-life example of the importance of subtle positioning differences.*

Let's look at an example from a small, regional 49er race in 2022.

On this track, the boat that we're looking at - let's call him George - won the pin end, and quickly gained an advantage on 3 boats on his hip.

The weather boat of the three - let's call him Larry - had a plan A start and hug on George's hip, while the other two were forced to bail out. After about a minute of sailing on starboard tack, Larry faded slightly into George, and tacked to clear. Seeing Larry tack out, George looked around, realized that he too wanted to tack to cover the fleet, and tacked about 10 seconds later.

As we can see from the speed graph, George was going 10 knots upwind just before his first tack, which means that by waiting 10 seconds to match Larry, George gave him about 40 additional meters of leverage. In a 5 degree right shift, this is an extra 3.5 meter gain for Larry, or 7 meters in a 10 degree shift. Assuming he is likely going to wait for a favorable shift to bring back, this probably negates George's lead on the next 10 degree shift.

In this case, George should have had the discussion much earlier, about whether he wanted to tack, so that when Larry tacked, the decision was already made. From there, the question is one of how strong George is. If George has a full boat length advantage or more, he may want to try for the tight cover from the Green example above. If he is moderately strong, but worried that a wind shift might be enough for Larry to get to his breeze in the tight lane, maybe he would take the purple lane. If he feels that he has made all of his gains due to a speed advantage despite being in right phase, or if he feels that there is more left shift left to come, maybe the red positioning makes more sense to take additional leverage to the left, but in this case confidence would need to be quite high, and one of the characteristics that we have discussed about edge out conditions is the inherent randomness and instability in the wind, so this is probably not a bet that aligns with your underlying thesis.

#### Edge Out Tactics From Front v. Middle; Early v. Late

One characteristic of edge out tactics is that they change slightly as the fleet thins out throughout the race. Let's dig into the differences.

*Figure 47. Subtleties in the weather boat positioning can make a big difference.*

In the illustration above, let's say you're the weather boat racing against Blue, and you've built a 1 boat length advantage. You're edging out left, and when the left shift happens, Blue suddenly starts feeling stronger - let's say they pull bow even - and tack back at you. You could call starboard, but if they decide to leebow, they'll be in a strong position to pinch you off, and you'll be forcing a split, which is not really what we want with our close competitors. Instead, the move here is to decide what you think the best weapon to attack might be.

In this illustration, Yellow tacks at the same time as Blue to maintain leverage and keep enough of a gap that she can tack back without getting pinned. This is a nice option because it maximizes your time sailing the lifted tack (you tack on the shift instead of before or after it), and because it keeps you bow forward enough that you don't worry about blue being a threat if the wind shifts more left.

In contrast, Green goes for a tighter leebow on Blue, which is a bit more of a match racing move. This is more aggressive, and gives Green the opportunity to start hurting Blue in just a tiny right shift. If you have separated from the fleet more - often later on in the race (second beat?) - and you're not worried about losing the boats behind you, this is a nice move because it can basically only go 1 way: you either hurt Blue, or you hold even. In contrast, giving Blue more leverage can allow them to escape into different pressure or a bit more lefty, which might give them the ability to turn the tables on you and put you in this same tight leebow position when you have to tack back.

Finally, Red sees the shift coming, and tacks quickly onto the shift. The communication in the boat before the tack was, **"As soon as I feel any left shift, I'm turning the boat."** Instead of putting the bow down in the lefty and then tacking, Red then is prepared to roll straight into the tack and opens up a bit more leverage. This is generally a nice move early in the beat, when the rest of the fleet on your hip is still an issue because you are able to bring strength back at them, while building slightly more leverage to gain on Blue when the next right shift comes. By doing this early in the beat, you ensure that there is plenty of run-way left for the right shift to come in (as opposed to late in the beat, where you might never get the righty to bring back at Blue).

**To be clear, there is not an initial VMG difference with any of these scenarios, but the tactical positioning changes the pressure on the boats around you in subtle but critical ways.**

#### Leverage And Risk

One of the ideas here that can be applied across any type of day, is that risk is a function of leverage, and that you want to adjust your strategy depending on where you are in the fleet. When you are winning the race, you can still play the edge out left strategy, but your willingness to give leverage to the boats you're racing should be lower. In contrast, when you're 3rd, or 5th or 10th playing the edge out strategy, you may be looking for a bit more leverage on the boats nearby, because you might be looking for the next shift to get you a bit more of an advantage.

Notice that in any case, I'm talking about pretty subtle differences. The difference between Green's position and Red's position, might be a total of 2, 3, or 4 seconds between when they tack, but that time and leverage can make a significant difference in the game of inches.

The better you get at this, the more you will find the position that both (1) keeps you executing your strategy for the type of day, while (2) scaling risk appropriately through leverage depending on where you are in the fleet. If you tack too close on your competitor you will not be able to execute your strategy, but if you give them too much leverage, you won't be able to control them and you give them a chance of getting back into the race with some good luck.

### Tactics of the Outside Track

When you identify the type of day as an Outside Track day, the tactical game becomes one of lane preservation and balancing how important it is to get to the favored side with how important it is to have a clear lane. In the earlier section on the outside track strategy, we discussed the importance of evaluating how much clear air is the right amount of clear air, but sometimes you need to tough it out in bad air for a while to get to the favored side. This is where it can really pay to have some tactical tricks up your sleeve. 

In particular, in this situation **blockers become a big part of the game**, and the next play will give an example of how a blocker might be used to ensure that your lane doesn't get taken away later in the beat.

#### UW 3.1 -- Find A Blocker To The Favored Side

**Guiding Principle(s):** Outside track.

**The Play:** Red should duck Blue and use her as a blocker to go to the left, that way there is no way for Yellow to bounce Red back right.  In this scenario, Yellow is essentially looking to execute UW 1.1, so the high percentage move accounts for Yellow's likely next move.  In this situation Yellow will likely lee-bow Blue and force them to tack out, or at least make it very hard for her to sail full speed.

If there is a line of boats above Blue that Red would have to deal with if she ducked Blue, Red should tack as early as possible to force Yellow to decide whether to tack or duck.  If Yellow lee-bows, then Red might have a chance to survive by double-tacking.

**Most Common Mistakes:** The obvious mistake here occurs when Red fails to account for Yellow's presence.  If Red lee-bows Blue, and then Yellow lee-bows Red, chances are good that Red (being the sandwiched boat) will get shot out the back and have to tack away.

**Pro Tip:** The key to making this play happen as effectively as possible, is knowing exactly how far past Blue you must sail in order to live on her hip.  Go too far and you lose leverage toward the favored side as well as control of when the other two boats tack, while not going far enough can result in an early exit due to bad air from Blue.  For the best chances of success, master your ducking maneuvers, as well as your tacks, and be sure to spend some time practicing sailing in a tight lane!

**Best Communication:** "Need a blocker back left," "Blue is our blocker," "Tacking to pin after Blue"

\***

In addition to using blockers on the race course, it is important to think about how to preserve your lane once you get to layline, as outside track race courses often necessitate early layline arrival. While the next play isn't exclusively an Outside Track play, it is an important one to have in the tool bag any time you get to layline early, and you'll often need to use it on the outside track.

#### WM 6.1 -- Live On Lay Line By Attacking Port Threats

**Guiding Principle(s):** Small gains at the mark rounding pay big in the escape
from the mark.

**The Play:** When you get to lay line early, any extra boat handling will result in a big loss -- not only will you be doing extra maneuvers, you'll be sailing extra distance as well -- so it's critical to manage nearby competitors correctly in order to live. The goal of this play is to avoid a brutal lee-bow, by creating space to leeward, buying yourself clear air, and the ability to live to the mark. In the best case scenario, you'll force your competition to either tack shy of lay, or go behind you, but even if they live to round the mark ahead of you, you'll be in a much better position for the downwind leg. The key here is building speed early with a bow-down mode. The more speed you have, the more difficult it will be for Blue to judge where they need to tack, which will generally result in them tacking early. Once Blue commits to the tack, burn your speed in a high mode, to lift back into a higher lane and create even more space.

**Most Common Mistakes:** Success here requires an aggressive mindset; the harder you attack the port tacker, and the more speed you can generate, the more difficult their life will be. This move can work in so many situations, but a tentative approach will only result in giving away gage on the mark. Bev aggressive and commit to your play!

**Pro Tip:** A good discussion to have before every race is how much risk versus reward is in each corner of the course. When current is pushing up course, and there is a current gradient across the course, often times, the corner with favorable current will be very high risk but very high reward. In these situations, getting to lay line early can pay big if you have the tactical tools to live, so be aggressive, and go for the big rewards, but use your tactics (definitely keep this play in mind!) to mitigate risk.

It can be slightly more difficult to make this decision when you are barely laying; putting the bow down might kill your chance of making the mark. The discussion that will help make the decision should be about how critical it is to sail in clear air. If you have a long way to go before you round the mark, clear air is king, but if you'll be rounding in the next thirty seconds, maybe it's better to suck it up, stay high, and let the lee bow happen to ensure you make it around the mark.

**Best Communication:**

"Incoming threat on port tack," "We need to live here; we're on lay line," "We have quite a while left on starboard lay line -- clear air is the priority," "Create a lane here," "Bow down - attack mode"

### Tactical Summary

As I said at the beginning, tactics deserve a whole book to themselves, but hopefully this section has helped you to understand how to better distinguish your tactical decisions from the strategic ones, and how to align your tactics with the type of day.

As with every other piece of race course decision making, each team will develop their own plays, communications, and archive of memories to draw from when making the split second reactions to situations around the race course. With that said, there is no substitute for sitting down with your teammates and talking through the intended responses before they occur to create a baseline system that you can build from. Do the homework and you will be rewarded on the race course!
