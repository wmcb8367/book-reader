As we can see from the speed graph, George was going 10 knots upwind just before his first tack, which means that by waiting 10 seconds to match Larry, George gave him about 40 additional meters of leverage. In a 5 degree right shift, this is an extra 3.5 meter gain for Larry, or 7 meters in a 10 degree shift. Assuming he is likely going to wait for a favorable shift to bring back, this probably negates George's lead on the next 10 degree shift.

In this case, George should have had the discussion much earlier, about whether he wanted to tack, so that when Larry tacked, the decision was already made. From there, the question is one of how strong George is. If George has a full boat length advantage or more, he may want to try for the tight cover from the Green example above. If he is moderately strong, but worried that a wind shift might be enough for Larry to get to his breeze in the tight lane, maybe he would take the purple lane. If he feels that he has made all of his gains due to a speed advantage despite being in right phase, or if he feels that there is more left shift left to come, maybe the red positioning makes more sense to take additional leverage to the left, but in this case confidence would need to be quite high, and one of the characteristics that we have discussed about edge out conditions is the inherent randomness and instability in the wind, so this is probably not a bet that aligns with your underlying thesis.

#### Edge Out Tactics From Front v. Middle; Early v. Late

One characteristic of edge out tactics is that they change slightly as the fleet thins out throughout the race. Let's dig into the differences.

*Figure 47. Subtleties in the weather boat positioning can make a big difference.*

In the illustration above, let's say you're the weather boat racing against Blue, and you've built a 1 boat length advantage. You're edging out left, and when the left shift happens, Blue suddenly starts feeling stronger - let's say they pull bow even - and tack back at you. You could call starboard, but if they decide to leebow, they'll be in a strong position to pinch you off, and you'll be forcing a split, which is not really what we want with our close competitors. Instead, the move here is to decide what you think the best weapon to attack might be.

In this illustration, Yellow tacks at the same time as Blue to maintain leverage and keep enough of a gap that she can tack back without getting pinned. This is a nice option because it maximizes your time sailing the lifted tack (you tack on the shift instead of before or after it), and because it keeps you bow forward enough that you don't worry about blue being a threat if the wind shifts more left.

In contrast, Green goes for a tighter leebow on Blue, which is a bit more of a match racing move. This is more aggressive, and gives Green the opportunity to start hurting Blue in just a tiny right shift. If you have separated from the fleet more - often later on in the race (second beat?) - and you're not worried about losing the boats behind you, this is a nice move because it can basically only go 1 way: you either hurt Blue, or you hold even. In contrast, giving Blue more leverage can allow them to escape into different pressure or a bit more lefty, which might give them the ability to turn the tables on you and put you in this same tight leebow position when you have to tack back.

Finally, Red sees the shift coming, and tacks quickly onto the shift. The communication in the boat before the tack was, **"As soon as I feel any left shift, I'm turning the boat."** Instead of putting the bow down in the lefty and then tacking, Red then is prepared to roll straight into the tack and opens up a bit more leverage. This is generally a nice move early in the beat, when the rest of the fleet on your hip is still an issue because you are able to bring strength back at them, while building slightly more leverage to gain on Blue when the next right shift comes. By doing this early in the beat, you ensure that there is plenty of run-way left for the right shift to come in (as opposed to late in the beat, where you might never get the righty to bring back at Blue).

**To be clear, there is not an initial VMG difference with any of these scenarios, but the tactical positioning changes the pressure on the boats around you in subtle but critical ways.**

#### Leverage And Risk

One of the ideas here that can be applied across any type of day, is that risk is a function of leverage, and that you want to adjust your strategy depending on where you are in the fleet. When you are winning the race, you can still play the edge out left strategy, but your willingness to give leverage to the boats you're racing should be lower. In contrast, when you're 3rd, or 5th or 10th playing the edge out strategy, you may be looking for a bit more leverage on the boats nearby, because you might be looking for the next shift to get you a bit more of an advantage.

Notice that in any case, I'm talking about pretty subtle differences. The difference between Green's position and Red's position, might be a total of 2, 3, or 4 seconds between when they tack, but that time and leverage can make a significant difference in the game of inches.

The better you get at this, the more you will find the position that both (1) keeps you executing your strategy for the type of day, while (2) scaling risk appropriately through leverage depending on where you are in the fleet. If you tack too close on your competitor you will not be able to execute your strategy, but if you give them too much leverage, you won't be able to control them and you give them a chance of getting back into the race with some good luck.

### Tactics of the Outside Track

When you identify the type of day as an Outside Track day, the tactical game becomes one of lane preservation and balancing how important it is to get to the favored side with how important it is to have a clear lane. In the earlier section on the outside track strategy, we discussed the importance of evaluating how much clear air is the right amount of clear air, but sometimes you need to tough it out in bad air for a while to get to the favored side. This is where it can really pay to have some tactical tricks up your sleeve. 

In particular, in this situation **blockers become a big part of the game**, and the next play will give an example of how a blocker might be used to ensure that your lane doesn't get taken away later in the beat.

#### UW 3.1 -- Find A Blocker To The Favored Side

**Guiding Principle(s):** Outside track.

**The Play:** Red should duck Blue and use her as a blocker to go to the left, that way there is no way for Yellow to bounce Red back right.  In this scenario, Yellow is essentially looking to execute UW 1.1, so the high percentage move accounts for Yellow's likely next move.  In this situation Yellow will likely lee-bow Blue and force them to tack out, or at least make it very hard for her to sail full speed.

If there is a line of boats above Blue that Red would have to deal with if she ducked Blue, Red should tack as early as possible to force Yellow to decide whether to tack or duck.  If Yellow lee-bows, then Red might have a chance to survive by double-tacking.

**Most Common Mistakes:** The obvious mistake here occurs when Red fails to account for Yellow's presence.  If Red lee-bows Blue, and then Yellow lee-bows Red, chances are good that Red (being the sandwiched boat) will get shot out the back and have to tack away.

**Pro Tip:** The key to making this play happen as effectively as possible, is knowing exactly how far past Blue you must sail in order to live on her hip.  Go too far and you lose leverage toward the favored side as well as control of when the other two boats tack, while not going far enough can result in an early exit due to bad air from Blue.  For the best chances of success, master your ducking maneuvers, as well as your tacks, and be sure to spend some time practicing sailing in a tight lane!

**Best Communication:** "Need a blocker back left," "Blue is our blocker," "Tacking to pin after Blue"
