Tactics and strategy are the most difficult topics in sailing to teach and to learn because you can make all the right decisions and still lose a race, and you can make all the wrong decisions and still win. As a result, it's easy to get confused about what is right, what is wrong, where you might have applied good tactics, but just made a bad big picture strategic call, or vice versa. 

The system that I'm going to teach you can't eliminate that, but it will teach you how to think about your own races to evaluate where errors occurred. To do this, it's helpful to keep a journal where you write about each race you sail in, and follow a repeatable, 3 step process for evaluating those races. 

**Step 1** is to write down observations from before the race: what did you see on the race course that might impact your game plan? 

**Step 2** is to write down what you thought those observations meant, or in other words, what your game plan was based on your observations. 

**Step 3** is to write down what actually happened, and to reflect on whether there were factors that you should have identified or weighted differently. 

Let's look at an example. 

*Figure 1. Looking upwind from an aerial view of the race course in Arhus, Denmark.*

Let's say we look up this race course in Aarhus, Denmark. The marks aren't set yet, but we immediately see puffy conditions. There is land close to windward of the course, and the breeze is very shifty. Once the marks are in the water, we recognize that the course is square and the starting line is long enough for all of the boats to fit. All of these observations are the things that we'll record in our notebook at the end of the day for step one. No opinions yet, just observations of what we see. 

Based on these observations, hopefully we made a game plan before the race, and step two of our journal entry is all about writing that game plan down, no matter whether it was right or wrong. In this case let's say, that after looking upwind, we thought that the course would be puffy and shifty, so we picked an "edge out" strategy - this is something we'll talk a lot more about when we get into the topic on strategy and type of day. We weren't sure which side to play, so we figured that a boat end start would be high percentage. 

One really important note here is that the edge out strategy has a built in plan B associated with it - if we get shot out at the start, we're still looking to work towards an edge - likely the right edge in this case because we're planning on starting at the boat end. 

We'll talk later about switching the plan at the last minute, and the risks involved there, but if you switched plans after the warning signal or any time during the race, it's important to note that in part two of your journal entry. With our plan A and our plan B clearly defined, we have a game plan that we can follow on the race course to see if it's right or wrong. 

**The biggest mistake that you can make on the racecourse at this point, is to allow yourself to get pushed around and not end up executing your plan**. In step three of your journal entry, you'll be recording whether the plan worked or not, so if you didn't actually end up executing that plan at all, then you're not going to learn much from this exercise. In this case, let's say we executed our plan A, "edge out right" strategy, and rounded the top mark in 10th out of 30, winning the right, but losing out to the pack on the left. Obviously there must have been some successes for us to round top 10, but in step 3 it's important to reflect on the fact that the left ended up paying and to ask the question, "Was there something I could have seen to predict that ahead of time? Was there a geographic feature or visual pressure on the water?" 

In this case, I might hypothesize that the windward shoreline actually created a left bend to the wind that was more significant than I realized. Again we'll talk about shore effects in a later topic, but the takeaway here was that I expected it to be a puffy, shifty, random course, and in reality I weighted this factor of the windward shoreline too lightly, so in the future maybe I need to put more faith in the left when the mark is close to a windward shore. 

This is just a hypothetical situation, and your own races will all be different, but practicing this process of note taking will make you much better at racing in the long run and help to give you a steeper learning curve to understand factors that others miss. If you do this for 15 regattas per year, with 10 races at each event, you'll have analyzed 150 races. Think about what a difference this will make after a year or two, or four, or ten!
